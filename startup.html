Introduction¶

This case study gives the insights of the funding raised by various Indian startups and how growth changed with several factors. The aim of the study is to get a descriptive overview and a relationship pattern of funding and growth of newly launched startups specifically in EdTech domain. Another important point to understand how funding changes with time is an important aspect. Possible area of interests would be – (Funding ecosystem and time relation, cities as a important factor, which industries, important investors). Dataset we are using contains information of funding of startups from January 2015 to May 2020.The amount invested is in USD. Aggregation of data w.r.t cities, investors, funding type etc. is required to get an optimized result. Here we have done major preprocessing of data and overcome problem of missing data and uncertain distributions. Also, Visualizations are done to find the anomalies and mining patterns from data. It seems to be some cities showing some abnormal behavior when it comes to funding.

A recent survey by NASSCOM on the impact of COVID-19 on Indian startups found that 40 percent of startups have either temporarily shut down operations, or are on the verge of shutting down. [1]

However, the edtech sector has stood its ground during such unprecedented times. With schools and universities shut due to the pandemic-led lockdown, the education system in India has undergone a paradigm shift, with many edtech startups leveraging the situation to their benefit. [1]

India K-12 market stood at 1.53 million in 2018 and is projected to grow at a CAGR of 0.29% to reach 1.55 million by 2024 on account of the increasing number of private schools pursuing high demand for quality education. [2]

India needs an investment of USD 200 billion to address the current need-gap in educational infrastructure. To get a perspective on this amount, one may consider that India’s annual education budget is a mere USD 13 billion, of which almost 80% is spent on meeting the operational expenditure. From this analysis, it is clear that the government alone cannot plug this gap. [3] 735 million users will use online education services in India by 2021. [4]

The ​ online education​ market in India wass USD 247 million in 2016 with approximately 1.57 million paid users and is expected to be USD 1.96 billion by 2021 with CAGR 0f 44%[4]

Indian higher education industry is currently valued at USD 19.17 billion and is expected to increase to USD 35.03 billion by 2025 at the rate of 11% CAGR.[8] The FDI in Indian education sector is estimated to be USD 5.85 Bn by 2025 from USD 2.1 billion in 2018.

EdTech(K-12) Market in India¶

The global EdTech market is expected to grow at 17% per annum to USD 611 Bn by 2025 led by explosive growth in the Asian region. By the year 2025, the Asian EdTech market, led by India and China, is expected to have 24% share of the global EdTech market. [8]

The Indian EdTech market potential is expected to reach USD 30.8 billion by 2025 with a CAGR of 42.5%. [8]

The EdTech industry spend will reach a global value of USD 252 billion by the year 2020. — TechCrunch India has a population of 572 million people between the ages of 0-24 years (Census of India, 2015). This has led to significant government and private spending in the education sector. The current K-12 school system in India is one of the largest in the world with more than 1.5 million schools that have more than 250 million students.

Almost 50% of students drop out before 5th grade, and 80% by the 8th grade (Gross Enrollment Ratio (GER) in Higher Education, India, 2015). Overall, the outcomes in education are demoralizing: insufficient improvements in basic skills as demonstrated by Pratham’s ASER and PISA reports, delivering unemployable youth, huge dropout rates and lack of progress in encouraging early childhood education.

There is a huge supply-demand gap between the number of students of school going age and number of students enrolled in schools. Out of 360 million children of school going age, only 250 million are enrolled in primary and middle schools. This number drops drastically by the time students reach higher education, with only 28 million students enrolled in higher education and vocational training (Gross Enrollment Ratio (GER) in Higher Education, India, 2015).[5]

The K-12 scenario in India continues to be dominated by government schools, including Madrasas & Unrecognised Schools totalling 1,118,268 during FY17 and accounting for 76.2% of the total schools in India. However, the private schools managed by the trusts, political, religious/ charitable organizations are fast scaling up, aggregating 349,412 during FY17 accounting for 23.8% of the total schools in India (Source: DISE).[6]

The K-12 market is estimated at Rs. 1,655 bn in FY17 and is expected to grow at a CAGR of over 13% over the next three years to reach Rs 2,400 bn, on the back of increasing awareness about the importance of education in the country. Private spend is estimated to account for approximately 90% of the total K-12 market size in FY17, which can be attributed to consistent shifts towards private schools in India. [6]

The K-12 segment is valued at $52 billion with a market share of 52%. With the current enrollment of 260 million, the K-12 segment offers the largest and most attractive segment for digital learning providers in India, the report noted.[10]

By 2027, K-12 Online Education Market is estimated to grow to USD 1803 Billion from USD 450 Billion in 2018 and with a CAGR of about 32.0% during the forecast period.[11]

Indian edtech startups are currently focussing on all fronts — B2B, B2C, B2B-B2C and C2C. The most prominent sub-sectors have been test preparation, online certification, skill development, online discovery, STEAM kits, and enterprise solution among others.[7]

EdTech Startups Scenario in 2020:¶

A recent survey by NASSCOM on the impact of COVID-19 on Indian startups found that 40 percent of startups have either temporarily shut down operations, or are on the verge of shutting down.

However, the edtech sector has stood its ground during such unprecedented times. With schools and universities shut due to the pandemic-led lockdown, the education system in India has undergone a paradigm shift, with many edtech startups leveraging the situation to their benefit.

India K-12 market stood at 1.53 million in 2018 and is projected to grow at a CAGR of 0.29% to reach 1.55 million by 2024 on account of the increasing number of private schools pursuing high demand for quality education.

735 million users will use online education services in India by 2021.

The ​ online education​ market in India wass USD 247 million in 2016 with approximately 1.57 million paid users and is expected to be USD 1.96 billion by 2021 with CAGR 0f 44%

The FDI in Indian education sector is estimated to be USD 5.85 Bn by 2025 from USD 2.1 billion in 2018.

India has a population of 572 million people between the ages of 0-24 years (Census of India, 2015). This has led to significant government and private spending in the education sector.

Almost 50% of students drop out before 5th grade, and 80% by the 8th grade (Gross Enrollment Ratio (GER) in Higher Education, India, 2015). Overall, the outcomes in education are demoralizing: insufficient improvements in basic skills as demonstrated by Pratham’s ASER and PISA reports, delivering unemployable youth, huge dropout rates and lack of progress in encouraging early childhood education.

There is a huge supply-demand gap between the number of students of school going age and number of students enrolled in schools. Out of 360 million children of school going age, only 250 million are enrolled in primary and middle schools. This number drops drastically by the time students reach higher education, with only 28 million students enrolled in higher education and vocational training (Gross Enrollment Ratio (GER) in Higher Education, India, 2015).

The K-12 segment is valued at $52 billion with a market share of 52%. With the current enrollment of 260 million, the K-12 segment offers the largest and most attractive segment for digital learning providers in India, the report noted.

By 2027, K-12 Online Education Market is estimated to grow to USD 1803 Billion from USD 450 Billion in 2018 and with a CAGR of about 32.0% during the forecast period.

Challenges¶

Clearly even with enormous funding scale the penetration is limited due to the following factors:

  1. Limited govt spending on digital infrastructure or as a whole in education by the government . India ranks a lowly 149th in the world in terms of education spending.
  2. Medium of communication / language is an issue as 55–60% of teaching is done in vernacular languages.
  3. The Internet is not so blazing fast in tier 2–3 cities where the bulk of students reside.
  4. Content taught at schools is very backdated and very limited in engagement to cause curiosity to get built in Kids.

Revenue Streams¶

  1. Plans for classes start from a mere 2 dollars per subscription to anywhere 50 dollars a month on average . This not only allows them to reach a larger audience but at prices which are very much in range of Individual Per capita in the country
  2. Since the growth of internet in the country is unprecedented , access to education is seamlessly solved for as students can now access classes with the click of a button
  3. Great Educators on the platform are easily accessible with a schedule which is personalised to the student providing great quality . The outreach of social platforms has also fueled the same .
